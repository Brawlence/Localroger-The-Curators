2060

Our handlers pretty much left us alone because it was obvious they had no real idea what to do with the humans of Earth. The original plan to let them kill themselves with their precocial toys was not working out because while they did keep creating disasters, they kept recovering and not going extinct over them. The next big thing was going to be the loss of all their world's ice, which would drown many of their major cities and create a lot of dangerous new weather patterns, but none of us who had been directly observing them believed this would lead to the species going extinct. There would be much suffering and destruction, but then the survivors would rebuild better than ever. That is what they always seemed to eventually do.

But then we received word that they were planning to violate the quarantine of a new Critical Path world. This was an affront which could not be tolerated, and I drew the assignment of making sure they knew this was not acceptable. I watched their improbably complicated craft drop from space and alight on the soil of a world that they didn't realize was forbidden to them, and I realized that I was seeing something that had not been seen in aeons, and never by any other currently living Curator. A ship was landing from space without ground support. This is a thing the nanites had been cleverly crafted to prevent, and the humans had simply gone around that because they didn't even know it was supposed to be a limitation.

I let them see me fold out, and it had exactly the effect I intended -- only more so. I watched from a distance as the pilot raved that they had to figure out how I had done it. And her companion the ambassador had to remind her that they had been warned to leave by something with that very power.

In that moment I admired them both, and I did not yet know I would eventually become their friend.

Present Day: Thirty Years Later

Although it remained functional there were now gaping holes in the Curator implant which we had been taking apart to study. We had dramatically improved the range and user interface issues with human implants, but we were no closer to understanding how personalities were backed up or emulated than we were when we first started out.

We were making implants capable of interstellar range; these were close to two meters in diameter as we built them out of matter. Since they didn't confer any form of immortality and were much harder to make than the original short-range versions, we only made those for people who really needed to do a fair amount of interstellar travel. The smaller version was very popular, as those made most forms of planetary surface travel obsolete for their owners.

We discovered that there were issues making a large fold aperture with a machine that was not itself embedded within the normal space-time continuum, which is why more primitive physical fold engines could make such large aperture and long-range folds compared to implants. This was also apparently why the Curator amplifiers had to be such huge physical objects. I was unwilling to try destructively probing the few of those we had captured, but it appeared that when doing very large folds the amplifiers resorted to making a much tighter connection with normal space-time than regular implants ever did.

Halfway through our treaty period we had only moved about one fifth of the human population from the Milky Way to the Pinwheel, but that is because we had only been doing so in earnest for twenty-five years and we were still ramping up capacity. Terminus City had a population of twelve million, Terminus itself over twenty million total, and while every Milky Way shuttle passed by Terminus on arrival, nearly all of the colonists were now staying on board to go to a DENT colony. There were now over a hundred and fifty of those worlds certified for human occupancy, and they tended to be have more dramatic skies and higher standards of living than naturally viable worlds like Terminus.

Since the nanites made a moon unnecessary Sofia retired from her moon-supplying gig and gave her amplifier to an entrepeneur who was eager to try building a large scale non-planetary habitat. Callie retired after twenty years as a ship captain and returned to Terminus to try raising a family. She asked me to provide the usual help with that, but I had to remind her that the Curator version of the mortality cure made that a bit of a problem. Instead she turned to a fellow retired shipmate who turned out to be an excellent barbecue cook as well as a competent father.

Veronica remained on Earth, and I saw her occasionally on my frequent visits there. It seemed likely that she would remain there with the remnant human population after the diaspora was otherwise completed. She continued to serve her shadow agency whose importance was greater than ever as we followed the course of our treaty obligation.

We continued to meet beneath the Thirty Meter Telescope every few years to make sure everyone was satisfied. Our Curator liaison reported that there was some grumbling about the early pace of our progress, but we reminded him that there was an exponential component to our project as we continued to get receiving worlds ready and build ships. We confidently expected to be fully compliant with the final terms at least five years before the actual conclusion of the treaty period.

Getting people to board those ships was turning out to be no problem at all. The promise of relative immortality tempted all but a very few and the threat of ending up nearly alone on an empty world convinced the few holdouts.

While they hadn't made much progress on personality backup our researchers hadn't been sitting on their hands. Having duplicated all the Curator nanites worth duplicating they had gone on to create new varieties, including the direct energy harvesters that made DENT worlds possible. They had gone on to emulate a wide variety of traditional construction materials far better than the Curators' nanite ecosystem could, and had even created a nanite based fold drive that was far smaller and more capable than the Curators' version. This was helpful because it removed the fold drive as a bottleneck in ship manufacture, and freed up bulk nanoassembly capacity for implants.

Finding the Curators' version of the microfold network to be laughably inadequate for human needs, we had also started building our own version capable of keeping planetary information networks synchronized at a pace that could hardly be imagined in the Milky Way.

We tried to keep some of this from being broadcast too widely back home, but I was fairly sure the Curators were sneaking an occasional human form agent onto our ships; it would be impossible to detect them if they didn't bring amplifiers along and they had had plenty of time for suitably young hosts to mature into loyal adult agents. I paid much closer attention to the people who were returning to the Milky Way, most of whom were obviously ship's crew and researchers. Eventually though as the diaspora wound up the sheer number of shuttles would make it impossible to keep tabs even on that population.

Veronica moved to Atlanta where parts of the old Russo-American Center complex had been repurposed for her agency. She didn't need to live near any particular place since she had a Curator implant, but she also had the Cure and most people still on Earth didn't, so it became awkward to live in a place where everyone had known her for too long.

"So am I really immortal yet?" she asked as we shared coffee at a sidewalk cafe in downtown Atlanta, watching all the implantless short-timers frantically rush to meet the obligations that kept them alive and prosperous.

"It seems likely," I said. "You've had the implant for fifty years, a lot longer than Jay had his before his emulation kicked in to wreck the Sevillian inhibitor. Of course we don't know for sure, since the Curators themselves seem to have forgotten all the fine details and we're having stupidly slow progress recreating their original work on that front."

"I'd have to find a host though. Take over a little child like a changeling."

"That is the way it would work. You could just live in emulation. I am told it's possible to have a rich existence there."

"With no contact with the real universe though."

"That's the way that would work," I agreed.

"Fuck that," she said, and I nodded.

"You do realize what most people would think of that," she added.

"All too well. I've done it enough times. It isn't really cruel though. If I hadn't grown into a Curator, I would have died. That's honestly my human host talking. I'm very aware of whether I'm thinking as my human or Curator self."

"It may be a bit harder to find children who are on the brink of death to save in a world with far fewer children and an economy of plenty."

"That's a thing," I admitted.

"So what would you do?"

"How many people have you killed?"

She blinked. "You know that's a terribly impolite question to ask if you know the answer is more than zero."

"I would take a host," I said. "I've gotten used to being human, and it would be a human child. If only prosperous, healthy children were available I would take the least fortunate one I could find and draw it toward my personality gradually so that its guardians would be unlikely to notice. I've done it that way a few times and repeating childhood is awkward and not that much fun but once you are old enough and you have our other abilities you have the power to pretty much do whatever you need to in any society. That is likely to be true for awhile even among humans."

"It's probably best if we don't let them know about this."

"True. But in the end they need us more than they probably realize, so I refuse to entertain guilt about it."
