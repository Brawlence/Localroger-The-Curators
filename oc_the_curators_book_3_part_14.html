1963

Our pact to help the humans of Earth ran into a snag pretty quickly. We soon realized that we knew no more about the technologies humans were developing than the humans did themselves. We were so far ahead of them that we had completely forgotten the basic techniques that were precursors of our own vastly superior technology. So we watched with genuine interest as they developed techniques of information processing that were within their reach to implement. It turned out that we knew no more about nuclear reactions than they did, because nuclear reactions are mostly useless once you understand how stars evolve and you have the fold. When the test bomb Castle Bravo ran away and harmed many of its observers, we were as surprised by the unanticipated side reaction as its builders.

It did not occur to us that chemically powered missiles could reach space, or that they could be used to deliver nuclear weapons with hardly any warning, until the humans demonstrated these things. As always those who had recruited us pointed to the resulting hair trigger situation as proof the humans would destroy themselves, but the thing we observed then was that the humans seemed to be aware of the problem too. When the human mathematician John Nash proved that the hair trigger could be made more stable, few offworld Curators could even understand his argument.

There was a period when our biggest worry was that an accidental failure might set off the hair trigger despite the stabilizing influences humans had inserted into their control systems. We did make preparations to throw the biggest spanner we could into the gears of their nuclear command structure if we had to, but there were only six of us and it was a massive enterprise. Fortunately we broke lucky and they began drawing down their overkill capability before anything like that had a chance to happen. They did come close a couple of times. We were prepared to intervene in the event they came to call the Cuban Missile Crisis, since there were only a few ships and missile installations directly involved, but it turned out that the humans present managed to avert the danger on their own.

We did not understand the dangers of nuclear energy because we had never had need to try to make use of it, and so the contamination and reactor accidents took us by surprise too. We also took note that they were altering their world's climate -- that was something we did understand -- but we could think of no way to alter their behavior without effectively Curating them.

Since there was a gradually evolving understanding among humans that making big war would be at best ineffective, the powers of Earth pursued other ways to gain influence and assert their geopolitical positions. The global superpowers interfered ruthlessly in the affairs of smaller nations, and as much as they dared with each others' politics through propaganda, blackmail, and other methods.

And like most humans who were alive then all six of us remember where we were on November 22, 1963 when we got the news that the popular and inspirational American president had been assassinated.

Present Day

Callie acquired a motorcycle and often when the Palomino was in orbit, meaning her services would not be needed, she would ride it to the outskirts of Sydney so as not to lead any fold effect snoops to her lair and her starship truck, unlock her implant's fold drive, and fold over to Denver to visit Veronica. The first few times she did this her clothes did not make the trip with her, but learning to overcome that bug was one of the reasons for visiting. That and the other thing; Veronica and I were also occasionally intimate, and she made it no secret that her voyage to the Pinwheel with Callie had involved more than just small talk.

For the first few years after the ultimatum in the major human population centers it felt like little was happening. But those of us who were in a position to do something useful were furiously busy. Her experience selecting moons made Sofia something of a goddess of the new school of DENT world design. DENT was a blank slate that could make almost any environment habitable, but it was also a glove that had to be slipped over an existing world. At some point someone had to decide where to put the cities, whether to use a basin for a lake, and how to skirt the mountain ranges. Nanites could do a lot but they weren't good at making that kind of decision. Sofia was training others as she learned the craft herself; while there might only be a half dozen new worlds so far, eventually the evacuation goal was to make a thousand, and their number would grow exponentially as the new bulk nanoassembly and nanite manufactories came online.

The Palomino was now always fully booked on voyages to the Pinwheel, and plans were being laid to start shuttling the Bellerophon and the Limiting Factor too. This still wasn't enough to make even a small dent in our evacuation needs; we would have to build a thousand giant fold ships too to get the job done in a century. But that's what nanites are for.

Most of the people who came to the Pinwheel at this early stage were old, and they came to get the Cure before it was too late. The news that emigration would also confer effective immortality made the program very popular, and while there was some grumbling that we should stay and fight it was minimal. We also got word that this astonished and infuriated the Curators, but our deal had said nothing about mortality.

About a year after the Mauna Kea summit I was doing my usual vigil as the Palomino arrived and a familiar face appeared out of the crowd. It was the new Keeper of Amplifiers who had accepted our treaty offer -- and he didn't seem to have an amplifier. He recognized me and came over.

"Did you forget something?" I asked.

"Oh no, I promise I am simply here as a tourist. Your government said that was okay."

"It is indeed. We are not ashamed of anything we are doing here. Care for a coffee?"

I showed him the city from the cafe overlook. "Not too impressive," I admitted, "But it's home."

"Oh it's very impressive," the Keeper said as he sipped his mocha. "Maybe not so much for what it is, but definitely for where it is." He pointed up at the moon, which people had taken to calling Aqua Bella. "Is your moon habitable? I can't imagine that your people would be able to resist the temptation to try."

"Not yet, but you're right, there are plans."

"You will need every habitable square centimeter if you're giving everyone the Cure."

"I can't speak of other races, but it's well documented that human fertility drops a great deal when we are prosperous. At this point we don't think imposed population control will be immediately necessary. And of course the Cure doesn't extend our early fertile period by much unless drastic interventions are made, so we don't see a short-term exponential crisis unfolding."

"We'll see," he said with another sip. "I was wondering if there might be any chance of catching up with my predecessor."

"We don't know exactly where he is," I lied. "I do know he is still alive because the way I sabotaged his implant it would return here if his host dies." This happened to be true.

"Why did you let him live if you killed our other agent?"

"He had honor. His beliefs were erroneous but he was acting on them out of duty. The other agent was positively ready to splooge itself waiting for the Earth to disappear as it watched out the window of a fold ship bar in orbit."

"I can see how that would have annoyed you."

"Immensely."

"We cannot figure out how you got the knowledge to do things like stealing our amplifiers or sabotaging our implants. Not that I expect you to tell me. There are some among us who are terrified about it."

"Worry would be justified," I said, "but only if you keep provoking us. We have no quarrel with you. We recognize your claim to the Milky Way as your garden and now that we understand how important it is to you, we intend to respect it."

"I appreciate that," he said, and I noted that he had not said we.
