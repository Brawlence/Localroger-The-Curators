Forty-Five Years Later

The Boardwalk Empire was the last ship to depart the human colony world of Providence for the new human colonies of the Pinwheel galaxy, making Providence the third human Milky Way colony to be fully depopulated and certified for return to Curator control. Places on the last voyage had been reserved years in advance, and some people had been waiting for decades to be able to say they were on that flight.

Her gravity vacuum skirt was loaded with cargo, from complete houses which had been built with authentic hand techniques and local materials to shipping containers full of tools, collections, prizes, memorabilia, and anything else which could comfortably survive a few days in the vacuum of space. These were the hopes and memories and future dreams of thirty-two thousand people, none of which would ever be realized.

Somewhere on the vacuum skirt -- its exact location will never be known -- a mechanism which had been scanned and determined to be inert on loading woke up when lack of air pressure closed a simple mechanical contact. After that the crystal sphere took notice of each of the Boardwalk Empire's fifteen hundred folds as it crossed the void between the galaxies, and when it sensed that one of those folds was followed by the nearby presence of a planet and star it executed its pre-programmed payload routine.

The global defense network which I had built for Terminus took note of this forming fold event, identified it as having an aperture diameter of twenty-one hundred meters which was perfectly ordinary for a fold ship of the Empire's class, and took no action.

A microsecond later the Boardwalk Empire no longer existed.

A tenth of a second later neither did anything which had been on the surface of the hemisphere of Terminus facing the Boardwalk Empire's station point. Seventy million people, a bit less than half of Terminus' population of a hundred and fifty million, died instantly and the regolith and oceans were vaporized to a depth of nearly twenty meters.

It was more like the fireball of a thermonuclear bomb than an impact so there was little solid material ejected into space to fall on the hemisphere which had been shadowed by the mass of the planet. But now a flaming hypersonic blast wave was racing across the surface, to converge inevitably on the antipode.

Of the six other fold ships stationed around Terminus at that moment five were instantly vaporized or melted. The Wall Street had been fortunately in the shadow of the planet directly opposite Boardwalk Empire's arrival station point and her captain realized what was happing in time to halt its orbital motion and keep it in the planet's shadow on supergravity.

She was also able to tell the survivors on Terminus what had happened. From the surface it was not immediately apparent why half the world had suddenly gone silent, although savagely bright light reflected from the stabilizing moon Aqua Bella was a clue. And she was able to warn them of what was going to happen next as her crew detected the circle of fire coming over the horizon.

She then activated a protocol which I had created and submitted to the drive manufacturers more than seventy years earlier. Potentially crowded venues like stadiums, theaters, and ships negotiated with human implant wearers for arrival coordinates and schedules, usually in dedicated fold entry foyers. The evacuation protocol began issuing arrival coordinates everywhere on the ship, carefully sorted to avoid collisions. The ship which had been made to comfortably carry thirty thousand people on a three day voyage could carry a quarter million in emergency standing room only conditions.

Terminus had a fairly traditional transportation grid, so implants were not a universal requirement as they were on some of the DENT worlds. Only about ten percent of Terminus' population had them, and most of those weren't capable of interstellar travel. Wall Street took on two hundred thousand refugees in a few minutes and left for the nearest habitable world to offload them. As it was leaving the Notre Dame and Chinatown appeared in answer to its microfold Mayday call.

The blast wave was traveling nearly a kilometer every two seconds, scouring and baking the surface as it passed. It was about an hour from Terminus City.

I went to find Callie. She was with one of her boys and his father. Her other son had gone offworld to follow in her footsteps as a ship captain. "Go away Andrew," she said when I appeared. "You can save yourself. Go. None of us have interstellar capable implants."

"I know," I said. "I need your help and I don't have time to explain."

She sensed the urgency in my voice, nodded, and took my hand. We both nodded a silent goodbye to her family. I folded us to her old homemade ship on its pedestal outside the palace complex.

"You can't be serious," she said. "There's no way this thing still works."

I disappeared briefly, then reappeared with her fold drive from the display case inside the palace. Then I folded us inside the Petunia which immediately lit up and booted its displays to welcome us.

"I've been keeping it maintained," I said as Callie socketed the fold drive. "No reason in particular, but I wanted to keep it available just in case. Just in case of something like what just happened."

She looked at me and said, "I can't leave my family here. I can't save myself and leave them to die."

"Callie, I need you to fly the ship. You built it. You're right, we can't save your family. We can't save most of the people around us. And I could save myself with my implant. But with your help I may be able to save a lot of other people."

Working as if in a trance she enabled the life support system and booted the fold drive. "Where to?" she asked with a dreadfully flat aspect.

"Pop us out about half a light-year. Any direction. We have to get clear of the blast."

She did as I asked, and we were safe in interstellar space far beyond the immediate range of whatever had destroyed our home. I went into a state of enhanced intelligence via partial emulation which I had learned to create when I need to work on something like a computer program. I sent signed and encoded microfold commands to the nearest defense networks, which included commands to relay them throughout the galaxy.

"What are you doing?"

"On a delayed trigger -- I can't do anything that would stop or slow down the rescue operation -- the command I just sent will turn all the defense networks in the galaxy into full fold inhibitors until they receive a countermand. We have to make sure this doesn't happen again."

"What did happen?"

"We stopped them from putting the planet in the sun," I said, "so instead they brought a bit of the sun to the planet. About a two kilometer diameter sample of the solar core according to the defense network diagnostics. At least I assume it was the solar core; I know it was an in-system fold but not the exact destination, but I don't know what else would release enough energy to vaporize the entire surface of a planet from twenty thousand kilometers away."

"Why?"

"That's what we're going to find out. I've done what I can here. You need to start the sequence to take us to Earth."

We would find out later that the fold ships coming in response to our Mayday signal managed to save over three million people ahead of the blast wave.
But Callie's family and my constant partner Sofia were not among them.
