by popular request, a prelude coda to Book 3: Part 13

"I've got it," I said to the Earth's chief executive, and I handed him the printout.

"How did you get it?"

"The Witnesses had an agent on one of the first worlds where it landed before the network started eating it. She forwarded it home encrypted with their priority encoding. They sent me a coded message letting me know they had it. I sent Callie in Petunia to collect it in person."

"The Curators can censor the microfold network?"

"Who do you think wrote the software?"

This is the statement of the Promethean people as drafted by our Council based on Public input and subsequently affirmed by 91 percent of our adult population, on the matter of the Curator Ultimatum to the Humans of Earth:

Point One:

The Humans of Earth have a story they have told for generations of four misfits who find themselves in a strange land ruled by a great and powerful Wizard. Each of these misfits is in need of something important, and together they seek out the Wizard to ask for its help. The Wizard intimidates them with an imposing visage of smoke and fire and booming sound, but their little companion animal pulls back a simple curtain to reveal an ordinary person controlling this fraudulent show. It turns out the only gift the Wizard really has to offer them is the knowledge that they have always had everything they needed within themselves; they just needed the strength and guidance to draw on what was always theirs.

You probably can't see where we are going with this, so we will elaborate.

Point Two:

When you came to our ancestors with your supposed Gift of the nanites, they saw that gift for lushly padded and bejewelled cage which it was. All of your further gifts, of longer life and better health and easy shelter and plenty to eat, were contingent on accepting the nanites, microscopic mystery particles which you admonished us not to even try to understand. The art of shuffling them around would be more than enough to keep us busy you assured us, even if we could never know how they really worked or what hidden controls over them you might be reserving for yourselves.

For thirty generations we have held fast that we are not interested in power which we do not understand and cannot control ourselves, and you have acted as if this is some kind of defect in our kind to be corrected at whatever cost might be necessary.

Point Three:

We understand that you created our world and guided our evolution, and we are not so ingrateful as to take that for granted. Thank you for creating us. You have raised the creation of species like ourselves and worlds on which we can thrive to an impressive art.

But the humans of Earth have another saying, "the child is the parent of the adult." Perhaps if you did not deny yourselves reproduction as the cost of your individual immortality you would understand this. The ultimate goal of parenthood is not to make compliant sycophants, but competent beings who can become successful parents in their own turn.

But while you call us your children, we note that you do not use the Galactic Common word "parents" to describe yourselves. Instead you take the label "Curators" as if we are exhibits in a museum -- or, as you call it, a garden.

But we are not exhibits; we are living beings, and despite your efforts to shape us our existence means nothing if we do not eventually prove your skill in the matter of our creation by confronting the Universe on our own terms. Who can even say whether your efforts are "successful" or not unless we have challenged our circumstances and earned a measure of success or failure?

Yes you created us, but why did you bother? It surely wasn't because we asked you to, since we didn't exist yet, and surely at some point the debt we owe you for the unsolicited favor of our own existence must be balanced.

Point Four:

When the humans M and J came to us and told us their tale, we were firstly astonished that there was a species you had treated to an even rougher involuntarily penetrative coital act than you had given us. But we were then even more astonished at what the humans had accomplished. And for the first time we realized that we might have made a mistake; while we were waiting for you to do the right thing and show us a path that did not leave us on your platinum leash, the humans had gone ahead and done everything themselves because they had the immense advantage of not even knowing that you existed.

They told us that we had strength. They praised our towns and our productive system as being superior to their own at our level of development. They offered what you had not, which was to share all their skills with us, even those that might raise us to parity with them in the fullness of time.

And they followed through, earning our gratitude and our respect by their honesty and commitment. Note that we do not say they commanded our gratitude and respect, because those things cannot be commanded; one can coerce their affectation, but only if one thinks that cowardice is gratitude and terror is respect. The humans, who are not shy about admitting that they were their own worst enemies when they didn't even know about self-important ancient wizards who think they own the galaxy, taught themselves those lessons early and at great cost.

When we began to deviate from the path they were trying to teach us they warned us that we were entering unknown territory where their ability to help us might be limited and failure might be the outcome, but we saw a shallow path instead of the steep staircase they had climbed and we wanted to see where it led. When we succeeded they offered us congratulation and encouragement.
Our fold drive was not as good as theirs but it was much easier to make, and that counts for a lot when you don't have the humans' long history of industrial accomplishment. Our fold drives might not be as good as those the humans make, but they are a lot better than the ones you wanted us to have.

By the way, hiding the fact that supergravity was possible, when you had even given your children gravity plating and microfold communication, was a brilliant cut of their hamstrings. It must really irritate your gonads that the humans let that particular secret out of its vault.

Point Five:

Our humanologists told us that the name human J had given our world was backward, because in human myth it was the god Prometheus who had given the humans fire, rather than receiving anything from the humans.

But after we built our fold drive an odd thing happened. The people of your world CI:174114 realized that with a combination of our direction and the powers your nanite civilization offered, improved fold drives were even easier to make than we had known. So it turns out that we showed your captive children how to spread the bars of those cages you made for them and walk on their own, and now every one of your worlds has a copy of those instructions in case they are interested. We have indeed filled the shoes of the human hero Prometheus. But not for the humans; instead we have filled that role for all of your other children.

It is hilarious to contemplate how this must frustrate you. All those aeons of cunning and manipulation and unimaginably advanced technology overrun by paper and glass and the keen interest of a few hundred individuals. It doesn't even require a global effort any more, and there's nothing you can do about it.

Point Six:

Well, almost nothing. We do fear you because we are not stupid. Your power is great and your recent actions show that your emotional maturity is not equal to your technological prowess. It is not out of the question that you might resort to genocidal purification of those who dare to defy you. The humans tell us that you once actually did do that, and the fact that it was a very long time ago is not very reassuring considering how you are acting today.

But we still defy you because we have no choice. We will not be your captive exhibits displayed in cages of your manufacture so you can brag of your prowess at creation. If we can help your other children to break out of their cages we will do that too. And if it means you eat our livers every day until we are rescued by Heracles, or that you simply kill us, we will at least suffer our fate as free beings who have willingly chosen our path.

This is a thing we don't think you understand about the humans, or about ourselves, or about the others who are now building our glass fold drives. There is no zone of comfort or island of safety as intoxicating as the satisfaction of having accomplished something difficult and useful. It is a feeling we wonder if any Curator has even known in the aeons since your ancestors invented all this clever piffle. After all, for all the worlds and species you have created, it seems to have been a long time since you have done anything new. And new things, even your own potential for procreation, seem to terrify you.

Point Seven:

And so you came to of all people the humans of Earth, who went in a single lifespan from steam power to space travel, and in one more lifespan to the stars, and you tried to impress them with a booming apparation in their council chamber. You did this to the species who created the story of the Wizard of Oz. If you don't go mad and kill every last one of your children and start life in the galaxy over from scratch people will still be laughing about this when the last star in the Milky Way goes nova.

As we have filled the shoes of Prometheus in our simple quest for freedom, let us now fill the paws of the small animal who pulls back a curtain. What disappointing and misshapen form will be revealed? How crippled do you have to be to think it is necessary to shape an entire galaxy to your will?

If we may offer some advice, we suggest that you all get as drunk as you can without losing the ability to fornicate and entertain yourselves with a coitally novel experience -- for example, one that does not involve the unwilling participation of other uninterested beings. And perhaps while you are distracting yourselves with those strangely unfamiliar sensations of actual pleasure, the rest of us can get on with our lives.

"That's going to leave a mark," the chief executive said.

"Well it would if it could get more than one hop before microfold routing killed it."

"How are they doing that?"

"Oh I know exactly where the hook is. I used the techniques I learned from human hackers to reverse engineer this thing fifty years ago. Earth, our colonies, and Prometheus are probably the only worlds in the galaxy where the Curators can't kill whatever content they don't like, because we built our own nodes instead of just letting the nanites do it."

"Well at least we can get it to our own colonies then. But it's a shame we can't get it out to everyone."

"Oh, I didn't say that," I said.

He just looked at me.

"There are other hooks in the software. There's a couple of rarely used priority levels that bypass the content inspection and could have it at every node in the galaxy within two hours."

"You could do that?"

"I could. There might be repercussions though. Once we use it that trick might never work again if they can disable it. I'm not sure whether they can make that kind of update remotely."

He handed me the printout and said, "On my authority, do it anyway."
