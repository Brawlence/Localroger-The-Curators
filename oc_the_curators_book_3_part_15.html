It wasn't like diaspora was a new thing for humans; in the ancient past large populations had abandoned entire countries that turned hostile or inhospitable, and in the climate crisis of the twenty-second century most of the human population abandoned Earth for its new colonies.

The ships we built to bring our population to the Pinwheel were ten times larger than anything that had ever been built, and they were capable of landing with their complement of thirty thousand passengers. We built two hundred of those ships in order to get fifteen billion people to the Pinwheel within our promised deadline, and we made the ships spacious and pleasant so that when the diaspora was over they could also serve as permanent homes for a few million people who might like a more mobile and interior lifestyle than planets would afford.

We repeated our summit with the Curator representative every few years so that we could report on our progress, which was much less visible in the first few years of the treaty, and to make sure that we were in satisfactory compliance with every term we had agreed on. There were no further fold attacks on human worlds and the representative expressed surprised pleasure at our enthusiasm and industry in the matter.

As we began to actually draw down the population of our colonies we had to honestly report that we didn't think it would be possible to forcibly remove everybody. To our mild surprise the Curator expressed understanding, and reminded us that they had no way to compel their own people to comply with such an edict.

But we had offered a handsome carrot in the form of the mortality Cure, although it visibly bothered the Curator that we were creating such a large population of potentially long-lived humans at the Pinwheel. And we matched it with a stick; when we were ready to fully abandon a colony we would sow a human-specific sterility virus there. Anyone who stayed behind could live out their few boosted centuries, but they would not be starting up another generation.

The first wave of DENT worlds became not just habitable but comfortable about twenty years into the term of the treaty, and by that time we had built fifty new great ships, most of which had been mothballed until we had new homes for our colonists to reach.

Twenty Years Later

Callie guided the Spy Who Loved Me into orbit around DENT experimental world 388. The ship was nanite based, as nearly all Pinwheel ships were since we had to build a lot of them in a tearing hurry, but Spy was a modest scout vessel with a crew capacity of forty. It was her crew's job to determine whether DENT worlds were ready to certify for occupation by colonists, and I was on board for a short while as an observer for our Imperial masters King Jay and Queen Emma of Terminus.

"All right people, let's get the diagnostics. It's been eight years since we visited this place, and from the big swivel chair it's looking pretty good," Callie said over the ship's PA system.

DEX-388 was a proper planet in its own orbit but like most such worlds it had only a tiny captured asteroid for a moon. It had resembled Mars in some other ways, being cold and dry and nearly airless but the nanites had fixed all that. Now there was quite a bit of green visible from space, and while most of that would be fast-growing mosses and lichens the ecosystem was poised to become richer once there were more nutrients in the nanite-processed soil.

One by one the habitability indeces came up green, until the board was completed by the fact that the cities were finished and ready for habitation. "My fellow humans, I believe we have ourselves a new world here," Callie said and there was shipwide applause.

It now fell to the crew of the Spy to name this new world, since it was not human nature to keep calling it 388 until its star went nova. This was a ritual that would involve the entire crew of the Spy for several days, as we were all required to supply at least one suggestion and then to participate in a voting cascade which would decide on three final candidates. Callie, as the captain, would then pick one of those to be the name of the world. A tradition started by Queen Emma herself stated that a name had to be chosen before the crew of the Spy began their final task, landing and on-site evaluation.

The naming ritual was cause for much partying and I found Callie watching over her crew, not really participating very much in the festivities, because her role as captain required a certain distance.

"Leadership seems like a lonely occupation," I said as I offered her a glass of champaigne.

She accepted it and said, "I don't know, I spent an awful lot of time in the outback waiting for the call that Earth was under attack. I couldn't tell anyone why I was there or what I was capable of doing."

"Or that you had one of the most valuable objects in the galaxy hidden away in one of your parts drawers."

"In the sex toys drawer," she corrected with a sharp laugh.

"And now your suction truck is on a pedestal outside of the royal palace, and your Ben-Wa balls are in an exhibit case where millions of people will see them."

"Yeah." She took a sip of her drink.

"You seem sad," I observed.

"Not really. It's just... you've lived a long time, how do you deal with the idea that maybe you've done the best and most important thing you'll ever do, and all you will ever do forever more is skate on the memory of something you did when you were thirty years old?"

"That is so human. Callie, famous accomplishment isn't a thing you work toward, it is a thing you stumble upon. Just because you never do something like saving the world doesn't mean you're less of a person than the person who did. It just means you were in the right place and time to have the chance. When it happened you acted with determination and valor and that was necessary and critical to the situation, but there were other humans who would have stepped up if they were asked, just as there were others who would have failed. You do your best in those situations you encounter and don't think about being remembered for it. Most of what you do in a long life won't be remembered. Even you won't remember most of it."

"I suppose there aren't any statues of you out there," she said.

"Not exactly." But then I told her the original story of Joseph, whose coat of many colors had been an embelleshment but whose advice to an ancient king had been very real.

Callie laughed. "Well I guess I'm not in the Bible," she said. "I just think of all those years stacked ahead of me thanks to the Cure, and I can't imagine what it will be like to live through all of them. Once in awhile when I'm talking to Jay or Emma they will get quiet and I'll remember what they've been through. They're only a couple of hundred years old and I can't imagine what it's like to be them. I must seem like a child to you."

"All living humans are like children to me," I said. "But there is no shame in that, because you are precocious and brave and unwilling to take no for an answer, and you've done things that much older beings and even civilizations have considered impossible. I have a million years of personal life experience and I'm considered kind of youthful by my own people. But who among us would build a starship out of a sewerage scow and pilot it to another galaxy? We all know what a bad idea that is and would refuse to do it."

"The agent was unthrilled too. She promised me that if we died alone in intergalactic space she would make sure I died first."

At that I laughed. "She got in your ship knowing that was a possibility but also knowing that people she trusted and had sworn to serve said you were capable of pulling it off. Veronica doesn't show her fear. For her to do so would often be suicide."

"She allowed me to see her fear," Callie said and this time with a little smile. "She made quite sure I knew what it felt like myself."

Eventually the festivities roared to a crescendo and after the ballots were winnowed her chief engineer presented her with the three final ballots. This would normally have been done by her first mate, but she was passed out in her cabin in bed with the ship's biotech consultant.

"Well we are not doing this," she said and she handed me the first ballot, which was marked FRODO.

She unrolled the second ballot. "This is marginally more acceptable, but only marginally. She handed it to me and I saw that it read YARBLE.

"Well it is a ball," I said.

She unrolled the third ballot. "Comedians. They're all fucking comedians." She handed me the third ballot and said, "Well Andrew, welcome to the new human world Yarble, whose future residents will probably have quite a lore about why their world is named after a testicle."

I unrolled the third ballot and saw that it read SCOOBY DOO.

"I think you chose wisely," I said with a slight grin.

"Is there anybody on this brick awake and sober enough to push the button that tells it to land at the spaceport, or do I need to do it myself?"

"I think I can manage," the chief engineer said as he shook his head and chuckled.

When we were alone again Callie turned to me and said, "What must this look like to you?" She gestured at the ballots.

"It looks like your people know how to have fun," I said. "And that's something I'm trying hard to learn for the first time myself. It's not as easy as you might think. You shouldn't try so hard to lose it. There may be a statue of you and your project may be mounted on a pedestal but don't tell me it wasn't also fun to build a spaceship out of garbage and spare parts which only you knew could not just fly, but fly here."

She nodded. "It was fun."

"So that's your answer to what to do with all those years you now have to live through. Have fun, and the other things will take care of themselves."
